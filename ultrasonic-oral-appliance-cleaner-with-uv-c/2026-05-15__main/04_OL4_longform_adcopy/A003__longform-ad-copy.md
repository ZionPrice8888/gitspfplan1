## FULL AD COPY

The cleaning tablets my dentist told me to use every day were doing the opposite of cleaning. They were making sure I never noticed how dirty my retainer actually was.

---

I'm 35. I've worn a retainer since I was 24. That's eleven years of nightly wear. And for most of those years, I used cleaning tablets. Polident. Efferdent. Retainer Brite. Whatever was on sale at CVS.

The routine was simple. Drop a tablet in a glass. Add warm water. Drop the retainer in. Wait 15 minutes. Take it out. It smells minty. It feels smooth. It looks clear.

That's clean, right?

That's what I thought too. For years. The tablets were minty and the retainer looked fine. I had no reason to question it. My dentist told me to use them. The box said "clean." The smell said "fresh." What else was I supposed to think?

---

Here's what was actually happening.

Those tablets dissolve and release two things: oxidizing chemicals and fragrance. The chemicals kill some surface bacteria. Not all. Some. The fragrance molecules coat the retainer in a minty-smelling layer.

So your retainer comes out smelling like a mint factory. And feeling slippery. That slippery feeling? That's chemical residue. Not clean. Coated.

And the bacteria living inside the microscopic grooves of your retainer? The ones too deep for chemicals to reach? They didn't go anywhere. They kept growing. Kept multiplying. Kept producing the sulfur compounds that cause the actual smell.

The mint just covers it. For a few hours. Then the mint fades. And the smell comes back. Because the source was never removed.

You weren't cleaning your retainer. You were perfuming it.

---

Then I found something the FDA published about these tablets. Persulfates. That's the chemical that makes them fizz and "clean."

The FDA issued a warning because persulfates can cause allergic reactions. Swollen gums. Throat irritation. Chronic night coughing. Some people had to stop using them entirely.

And here's the part that got me: the industry knows this. The warnings are on the box, in tiny text, under the ingredients nobody reads. But dentists still recommend them. Stores still sell them. And we still buy them. Because what else are you going to do?

I was paying about $8 a month. That's $96 a year. For something that doesn't clean and might be causing an allergic reaction I didn't even know I was having.

---

The reason tablets don't clean isn't a quality issue. It's a physics problem.

Cleaning tablets dissolve chemicals into water. Water sits in a glass. The chemicals spread through the water. They contact whatever surfaces the water touches.

But your retainer has thousands of microscopic grooves, crevices, and channels molded into the plastic. They're what hold it in place on your teeth. They're also where bacteria live.

Those grooves are too small for water to circulate through. The chemicals float on the surface. They can't push into the grooves because there's no physical force driving them in. So the bacteria in those grooves sit there, untouched, while the surface gets a chemical bath and a minty perfume spray.

The only thing that can physically reach inside those grooves is something smaller than the grooves itself. Something that can get in, pull the bacteria out, and carry it away.

That's where ultrasonic cavitation comes in.

---

At 45,000 vibrations per second, the water in an ultrasonic cleaner creates millions of microscopic bubbles. These bubbles are small enough to fit inside those grooves. When they implode, they create a tiny vacuum that pulls bacteria, biofilm, and debris out.

Physically pulls it out. Not chemically. Not with fragrance. With force.

Then UV-C light hits the surface and deactivates whatever organisms are left.

The result is something tablets can never produce: a retainer that's actually clean. Not minty. Not slippery. Just clean. You can tell because the water shows you the proof.

---

I got one on Amazon. About 60 bucks. It arrived on a Thursday.

That Sunday evening, I dropped my retainer into it. The same retainer I had soaked in a Polident tablet that morning. I filled it with tap water. Pressed start.

Five minutes later, I pulled the retainer out.

The water was brown. Dark brown. Cloudy. With little particles floating in it.

That brown water came from my retainer. The same retainer that had been "cleaning" in a tablet all day. The same retainer I was about to put back in my mouth that night.

Elaine Pandolfe, another former tablet user, described the same thing: "Bleaching tablets... after cleaning, they still felt slippery and there were still blind spots. The ultrasonic cleaner actually performs real physical vibration to remove dirt."

Another reviewer, Babycakes, said: "My guards are clean and I don't have the overly minty taste in my mouth."

That minty taste? That was the perfume. Not the clean.

---

Within a week, my retainer didn't smell like mint anymore. Because it didn't smell like anything. That's what actually clean smells like. Nothing.

No chemical aftertaste. No slippery film. No waiting for the mint to wear off so the real smell could come back.

I haven't bought a single box of cleaning tablets in six months. That's $48 I didn't spend. In another three months, this machine has paid for itself.

---

If you're using cleaning tablets right now, I'm not asking you to take my word for it. Try this: soak your retainer in a tablet tonight like you always do. Tomorrow, drop it in an ultrasonic cleaner with plain water. Press start. Wait five minutes.

Look at the water.

Then decide what "clean" means to you.
